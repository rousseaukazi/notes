Kill your identity
before it gets murdered

The market is coming for who you are.
Do it yourself first.

scroll

The title of this post is probably the most frequent piece of advice I've been sharing with my friends and family over the last couple of months.

A threat to one's identity is one of the scariest things we can face. Getting injured and no longer being an athlete. Getting broken up with and no longer being a boyfriend. Getting fired from your firm and no longer being a lawyer.

For many of us, our identity is who we are. We spent so much of our lives building finding it. If it gets taken away, we lose our purpose, we lose our value, we lose... well, us.

Your identity is under attack

If you're reading this, there's a good chance you're in tech or do some sort of knowledge work. You get paid to produce certain creative outputs (code, pixels, words, numbers) that push a mission forward.

You're probably really good at what you do. You spent years honing your craft, getting praised for your creativity and execution, and you take pride in being better at your thing than a hefty majority of the world.

Given just how good you are, you honestly probably realized a while ago that AI was going to be better than you at your thing, and recently, that it already is. "Better" can be subjective, but practically it means the same or higher quality output, done in 1/100th the time, at 1/1000th the cost. In the most capitalistic definition, it's just better.

Anthropic's Opus 4.6 is easily a better engineer than 99.99% of engineers you'll ever meet. What we know as knowledge work is becoming highly commoditized, and so is your comparative advantage. Tech is becoming fully democratized, and even though that's objectively a good thing, it's hard not to feel some mourning for what we've worked hard to achieve.

So what do we do?

Kill your identity...

It's natural to want to fight what's happening. It still makes mistakes! IDK... still feels kinda slow. You need humans to write this stuff because it just feels better when they do.

There's a reason it's called an uncomfortable truth. It sucks to accept.

Yeah, it hurts but you'll be fine. Just do it. It's not as precious as you think. You've gone through plenty of identities as a human, and it's happened faster than you realize.

We all know that person who never worked out, signed up for a 5k, sorta got into it, started posting about their jogs, and within a few months it became a critical part of their identity. That's how fast identity shifts.

Humans are infinitely unique and interesting.

AI will forever be a tool for us to live a more full life. The faster you let it be better than you at certain things, the sooner you'll free yourself to do what you uniquely do. And the sooner you'll go from being scared of its capabilities to annoyed that it can't do even more.

If I'm no longer a {job}, then who am I?

One of the things that has always pissed me off about our industry is the heavy typecasting.

I'm an {engineer}, so I can't have an opinion on the {marketing website}.

I'm a {marketer}, so I can't have an opinion on {the mobile app's shitty scroll performance}.

...

It's insane. When I was running my startup, I would have the same conversation with every new hire that went something like this: "You're not a {function}, you're a problem solver who happens to be good at {function}. If all you do is focus on your {function} and don't think about the larger problem, then we're getting a fraction of the value you can provide."

The beautiful thing about AI is that it's making anyone who wants it a problem solver who happens to be good at {everything}.

Who are you? You are who you've always been. Someone who encounters a problem and thinks "fuck yeah" because it'll be fun to figure out.

Pessimists will view AI as a destructive force and will only see what's been taken away. Optimists will see all the time and energy they're gaining.

You have a team of geniuses at your fingertips looking for guidance.

"Give me a lever long enough and a fulcrum on which to place it, and I shall move the world." — Archimedes

AI is the greatest lever of our lifetime.

My favorite quote. AI is the greatest lever of our lifetime. Your identity should be the best operator of that lever — not someone bitter about the lever being better at lifting heavy things (which probably saved your back in the process).

Tactically: get really good at leveraging AI. Play with every tool you can, push it to its creative limits, try to do crazy things with it, and learn where it's magic and where there's room to grow.

Do it now.

...before it gets murdered

I honestly believe you'll be okay regardless of whether you take this advice or not. You'll be fine because you're incredibly resilient and I'm a relentless optimist.

I just hope you make the change before the market forces you to. Why? Because there's power and dignity in doing it proactively. You get to redefine who you are on your own terms.

From the examples above:

  • It's better to notice that you're getting older and retire from a sport before getting injured and bitter.
  • It's better to notice that you're not happy in a relationship and end it on civil terms before getting broken up with and feeling powerless.

When you do things on your own, you know you want it. When things happen to you, it's hard to know how much you want it vs. how much you're rationalizing. Save yourself from that doubt and do it now.

Okay, I'm now a problem solver who happens to be good at {everything}, but...

...what if AI actually does get good enough to solve all problems? What will I do to be happy then?

Well, that sounds like a problem, and I know just the person to solve it.

Fuck yeah.
